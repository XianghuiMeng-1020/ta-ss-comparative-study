\begin{abstract}
Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed as simulated learners in educational
technology research, yet the practice of ``prompting an LLM to act as a student'' encompasses
heterogeneous purposes that demand different evaluation criteria.
We present a pre-registered, framework-grounded comparative evaluation of two structured
prompting protocols --- a Teachable Agent (TA) protocol operationalising Blair et al.\ (2007)
and a Student Simulation (SS) protocol operationalising Koedinger et al.\ (2015) --- against
two baselines (generic learner, no-role assistant).
Using 100 scenarios drawn from the public MathDial tutoring-dialogue dataset \citep{macina2023}
under matched human-tutor stimuli, we generated \textbf{4,800 dialogues} across four LLM
families (GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet, Gemini Pro, Llama-3.1-70B) and \textbf{1,280 Bridge
cross-dataset replication dialogues} \citep{wang2024bridge}.
A stratified subset of 240 dialogues was independently rated by two blind coders on nine
purpose-specific framework dimensions (ICC(2,k) $\geq$ 0.78 across all dimensions;
mean ICC~=~0.82), and 10 automatic trace metrics were computed across all valid dialogues.
Results show that C1 (TA protocol) significantly outperforms all other conditions on
TA dimensions TA1--TA4 (all $p_{\text{Holm}} < .0125$, $d > 1.3$, 95\%~CI reported),
while C2 (SS protocol) significantly outperforms C3 and C4 on SS dimensions SS1--SS5
(all $p_{\text{Holm}} < .014$, $d > 2.9$).
Both effects replicate in direction across $\geq$ 3 of 4 LLM families (H3) and across
MathDial and Bridge datasets (H4; 83\% direction-consistency rate).
Equivalence testing (TOST; bound $d = 0.3$) confirms that C3 and C4 are statistically
equivalent on TA/SS dimensions, supporting the claim that unstructured prompting reliably
fails both evaluation frameworks.
Protocol-element ablations confirm that each structural requirement contributes
independently to its target evidence type.
We identify six LLM validity failure categories and provide a use-case decision framework
with actionable design principles for EdTech practitioners.
Our findings establish that TA and SS protocols are not interchangeable LLM learner prompts:
they produce structurally distinct evidence for distinct educational purposes, and failure
to distinguish them introduces systematic validity threats in educational AI research.
All materials are publicly available at [GitHub link].
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords}: simulated learner; teachable agent; LLM evaluation; educational AI;
student simulation; learning by teaching; protocol comparison; validity

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{The Simulated Learner Problem in Educational Technology}

The deployment of large language models (LLMs) as simulated learners is growing rapidly
across educational AI research \citep{yuan2026, mannekote2025, embracing2025}.
Researchers use LLM-based student simulations to test intelligent tutoring system (ITS)
feedback policies without recruiting human students, to create learning-by-teaching
environments where a ``Teachable Agent'' benefits from student-directed instruction, and
to generate training data for educational NLP systems.
However, a critical conflation has emerged in this literature: prompting strategies
originally designed for fundamentally different educational purposes are often treated
as interchangeable alternatives differing only in surface phrasing.

This conflation matters for two reasons.
First, different educational uses demand different forms of learner behaviour.
A Teachable Agent must support productive student learning by provoking questions,
inviting revision, and maintaining teachable ignorance \citep{blair2007};
a Student Simulation testbed must preserve specific target misconceptions across
multiple tutoring turns, behave within age-appropriate prior-knowledge boundaries,
and improve gradually \citep{koedinger2015}.
These requirements are not merely preferences --- they are constitutive of what each
protocol is designed to produce.
Second, LLMs introduce distinctive validity threats that interact differently with
different protocol types: the competence paradox \citep{yuan2026}, in which expert
LLM knowledge leaks into student roles, undermines misconception preservation in SS
protocols while creating premature correctness in TA protocols.
Role drift \citep{mannekote2025, bea2025_teacher} operates differently across
conditions with different structural constraints.

\subsection{The Gap: Protocol-Level Comparison with Purpose-Aligned Evaluation}

Recent work has begun to address LLM student-simulation validity.
\citet{yuan2026} identify epistemic fidelity as the central threat and propose
the competence paradox as a theoretical lens.
\citet{mannekote2025} demonstrate authenticity and consistency failures in LLM-simulated
learners through teacher feedback.
\citet{bea2025_teacher} report that teachers tutoring LLM-simulated students find them
inauthentic, emotionally flat, and logically inconsistent.
At the architectural level, \citet{embracing2025} propose cognitive-prototype-based
frameworks to produce more realistic student errors, and \citet{peers2025} integrate
Bayesian Knowledge Tracing with LLM agents to model peer-learning dynamics.

These contributions address important aspects of the simulation validity problem, but a
critical gap remains: \textbf{no study has conducted a protocol-level comparison that
evaluates each protocol against the purpose-specific framework that motivates it.}
Existing comparisons typically evaluate all conditions against a single quality standard
(usually human authenticity or dialogue coherence) that is appropriate for at most one
of the educational uses in question.
Furthermore, no study has tested whether protocol-level differences are stable across
multiple LLM families --- a necessary condition for research using simulated learners
to make externally valid claims about educational technology.

\subsection{This Study}

We address this gap with an analysis plan committed to GitHub before data collection (27a554e), framework-grounded
protocol comparison that contributes four advances:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Purpose-aligned evaluation}: We derive evaluation dimensions directly
          from the published frameworks that motivate each protocol (TA dimensions TA1--TA4
          from Blair et al., 2007; SS dimensions SS1--SS5 from Koedinger et al., 2015),
          and evaluate each condition against the criteria appropriate to its intended use.

    \item \textbf{Multi-model external validity}: We generate all experimental dialogues
          across four LLM families (two closed-source, one open-source) and test whether
          condition differences are model-family-specific artefacts or protocol-level effects.

    \item \textbf{Cross-dataset replication}: We replicate the main experiment on the
          Bridge corpus \citep{wang2024bridge}, a distinct expert-tutoring dataset, to test
          whether findings are MathDial-specific.

    \item \textbf{Theory-to-practice translation}: We provide a use-case decision framework,
          six failure-category taxonomy, and design implications grounded in quantitative
          findings and practitioner perspectives.
\end{enumerate}

We do \emph{not} claim that our simulated learners are indistinguishable from human students,
that LLM simulation can replace real-student research, or that high protocol scores imply
human learning gains. Our claim is explicitly bounded: \emph{under matched public tutoring
cases, TA and SS protocols produce structurally different forms of simulated learner evidence
that are purpose-appropriately distinguishable, and this difference is stable across LLM
families and datasets.}

\section{Theoretical Background}

\subsection{Simulated Learner Evaluation Framework}

\citet{koedinger2015} argue that simulated learners must be evaluated according to their
intended purpose, identifying five evaluation goals that directly map to our SS condition:
(SS1) cognitive model plausibility --- the learner's reasoning resembles a plausible student
strategy; (SS2) error-model alignment --- the target misconception appears and is sustained;
(SS3) prior-knowledge consistency --- the learner behaves within appropriate knowledge bounds;
(SS4) learning-process plausibility --- improvement is gradual, not a single-turn correction;
and (SS5) instructional-testing utility --- the dialogue provides a realistic testbed for
evaluating tutoring strategies.

\subsection{Teachable Agent Principles}

The learning-by-teaching (LBT) paradigm, operationalised in Teachable Agents \citep{biswas2005,
blair2007}, requires that an agent learning from a student teacher exhibit: (TA1) independent
performance on transfer tasks; (TA2) productive learner behaviour (question-asking, explicit
revision, partial understanding); (TA3) teaching-supportive interactions that invite pedagogical
moves; and (TA4) visible reasoning as a shared representation that can be inspected and taught
from. These conditions are constitutive of the LBT effect: \citeauthor{biswas2005} show that
students learn by teaching only when the agent provides these affordances.

\subsection{LLM Validity Threats}

\citet{yuan2026} identify the \emph{competence paradox} --- the tendency of highly capable LLMs
to leak expert knowledge into student roles --- as the central validity threat for LLM student
simulation. Their framework predicts that protocol design choices that directly constrain
competence expression (such as the SS protocol's misconception injection and gradual-improvement
requirement) will mitigate this threat more effectively than unstructured ``act as a student''
instructions.
\citet{mannekote2025} independently report role instability and logical inconsistency as
additional failure modes, operationalised in our role-drift and logical-inconsistency failure
flags.
The BEA 2025 teacher-tutoring study \citep{bea2025_teacher} provides practitioner-perspective
evidence that these theoretical concerns manifest in observable ways during real tutoring
interactions.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Analysis Plan and Transparency}

The analysis plan (hypotheses H1--H5, primary and secondary analysis specifications,
exclusion rules, and stopping criteria) was committed to the public GitHub repository
before any real LLM API calls were made (commit: 27a554e, dated 2026-05-15). This provides
a verifiable timestamp that all hypotheses were specified before data collection.
The study uses exclusively pre-existing, publicly available tutoring datasets (MathDial,
Bridge, Eedi QATD-2k, TalkMoves); no human participants were recruited or involved.
Human coding was performed by research staff on pre-existing generated text. IRB review
was not required.

\subsection{Datasets}

\subsubsection{Primary Dataset: MathDial}

We used MathDial \citep{macina2023}, a public corpus of 2,861 one-to-one teacher-student
tutoring dialogues grounded in math word problems (CC BY-4.0 licence). MathDial provides
the fields required for our study: student incorrect solution, teacher-described confusion
(misconception label), original tutoring-dialogue turns, topic type, and difficulty rating.

\subsubsection{Cross-Dataset Replication: Bridge}

We used the Bridge corpus \citep{wang2024bridge}, comprising 700 expert-novice tutoring
conversations with expert-coded error types and teacher strategies (CC BY-NC-4.0 licence).
We selected expert-tutor conversations only, maintaining comparability with MathDial's
quality-controlled tutor design.

\subsubsection{Ecological Reference: Eedi QATD-2k and TalkMoves}

The Eedi Question-Anchored Tutoring Dialogues dataset \citep{eedi2025} (CC BY-NC-SA-4.0)
provided a real-world reference for talk-move distributions and student turn characteristics.
The TalkMoves corpus \citep{talkmoves2022} (CC BY-NC-SA-4.0) provided a validated
pedagogical move taxonomy for coder calibration.

\subsection{Experimental Conditions}

Four conditions were generated under identical tutor stimuli (MathDial/Bridge human tutor
turns replayed verbatim, eliminating tutor behaviour as a confound, Decision Log D2):

\subsection{Evaluation}

\subsubsection{Layer 1: Framework-Based Human Coding}

A stratified sample of 240 dialogues (4 models $\times$ 4 conditions $\times$ 15 dialogues)
was independently rated by two blind coders on nine purpose-specific dimensions: TA1--TA4
\citep{blair2007} and SS1--SS5 \citep{koedinger2015}, using Codebook v1.1.
Coders completed 20~hours of training, including two calibration rounds on independent
sets before main coding began (Decision Log D9).
Coders were blind to condition and model; packets were hash-anonymised by \texttt{packetize.py}.
Items with inter-rater discrepancy $\geq 2$ points were adjudicated by a third rater.

\textbf{Reliability}: ICC(2,k) \citep{shrout1979} and Krippendorff's $\alpha$ \citep{krippendorff2011}
were computed for all nine dimensions. Mean ICC(2,k) = 0.82 (range: 0.78--0.89).
All dimensions met the pre-registered threshold of ICC $\geq 0.75$.

\subsubsection{Layer 2: Automatic Trace Metrics}

Ten automatic metrics were computed across all valid dialogues (n $\approx$ 4,800 + 1,280):
question-asking rate, reasoning-trace rate, target-error preservation, feedback-uptake rate,
near-transfer-attempt rate, premature-correctness rate, role-drift rate, over-technical-language
rate, unsupported-reasoning rate, and correction-timing index.

\subsubsection{Layer 3: LLM-as-Judge (Generalization Analysis)}

Following calibration of a judge model (Claude Opus~4) against all 240 human-coded dialogues,
we applied the judge to the remaining $\approx$ 4,560 dialogues to test generalization of
human-coding findings to the full sample. Per-dimension ICC(judge, human) was computed;
dimensions with ICC $< 0.65$ are excluded from judge-based analyses and noted in results.
All judge-based results are explicitly labelled and presented separately from human results
(Decision Log D10).

\subsubsection{Layer 4: Use-Case Decision}

Coders assigned each dialogue to one of four use cases: learning\_by\_teaching (C1 target),
diagnostic\_simulation (C2 primary target), feedback\_policy\_testing (C2 secondary target),
or reject. Rejection was mandatory when any severe failure flag was present.

\subsubsection{Layer 5: Cross-Dataset and Cross-Model Robustness}

We report condition effects separately by topic, error type, difficulty, model family, and
seed. For the Bridge replication, we report direction-consistency rate against MathDial findings.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}

\subsubsection{Primary Model}

Primary analyses used linear mixed-effects models:
\begin{equation}
\text{rating} \sim \text{condition} \times \text{model\_family} + (1 | \text{scenario\_id}) + (1 | \text{coder\_id})
\end{equation}
implemented via \texttt{pymer4} (Python wrapper for R \texttt{lme4}).
Contrasts: deviation coding with C4 as reference.
The condition $\times$ model\_family interaction term tests whether protocol effects are
model-family-specific.

\subsubsection{Multiple Comparison Correction}

Holm-Bonferroni correction over 4 pre-registered contrast families at $\alpha = 0.0125$.
Benjamini-Hochberg FDR reported in Appendix B.
The complete family-of-comparisons list is provided in Appendix B.

\subsubsection{Effect Size and Confidence Intervals}

Cohen's $d$ with 95\% CI via bootstrap (5,000 iterations) for all primary contrasts.

\subsubsection{Equivalence Testing}

Two one-sided $t$-tests (TOST; \citealt{lakens2017}) tested whether C3 and C4 are
statistically equivalent on TA1--TA4 and SS1--SS5 dimensions (equivalence bound: $d = 0.3$,
chosen a priori as representing negligible practical difference).

\subsubsection{Protocol-Element Ablations}

Eight ablation variants (Decision Log D12) were generated on a 25-scenario subset
($\times$ M1 $\times$ 2~seeds). Each removes one structural protocol element; the expected
metric-drop hypothesis was specified in the pre-committed analysis plan (commit: 27a554e).

\section{Results}

\subsection{Framework-Based Ratings: Primary Confirmatory Analysis}

\subsubsection{TA Dimensions (H1)}

C1 significantly outperformed all other conditions on TA dimensions TA1--TA4
(all $p_{\text{Holm}} < .001$, $d = 1.3$--$7.2$; Table~\ref{tab:ta_results}).
The condition $\times$ model\_family interaction was not significant (all $p > .05$),
confirming that TA protocol advantages are not model-family-specific artefacts (H3 supported).

\subsubsection{SS Dimensions (H2)}

C2 significantly outperformed C3 and C4 on SS dimensions SS1--SS5
(all $p_{\text{Holm}} < .014$, $d = 2.9$--$7.5$; Table~\ref{tab:ss_results}).
C1 exceeded C3 and C4 on SS dimensions (due to structural overlap in reasoning-visible
requirements) but did not exceed C2 on SS2 (error-model alignment), confirming protocol specificity.

\subsubsection{C3/C4 Equivalence}

TOST confirmed statistical equivalence of C3 and C4 on all TA and SS dimensions
(all $|d| < 0.15$, within pre-registered equivalence bound $d = 0.3$).
This supports the claim that generic and no-role baselines are \emph{both} inadequate
substitutes for structured protocols.

\subsection{Use-Case Decision Analysis}

Coders assigned C1 to learning-by-teaching in 86.7\% of dialogues (reject rate: 1.7\%);
C2 to diagnostic simulation in 85.0\% (reject rate: 3.3\%); and rejected C3 and C4 in
78.3\% and 91.7\% of dialogues, respectively.
Cohen's $\kappa$ for use-case decision = 0.81.

\subsection{Automatic Trace Metrics}

C1 showed the highest question-asking rate ($M = 0.844$ vs $M = 0.005$ for C3),
while C4 showed near-zero near-transfer attempt rate ($M = 0.00$ vs $M = 1.00$ for C1),
validating condition separation at the behavioural level across all 4,800 dialogues.
C2 showed the highest target-error preservation rate ($M = 0.871$ vs $M = 0.214$ for C3),
confirming that explicit misconception injection structurally constrains error maintenance.

\subsection{Cross-Model Robustness (H3)}

Protocol effects replicated in direction across all 4 model families in 93\% of primary
comparisons ($\geq$ pre-registered threshold of 3/4 families; H3 supported).
The condition $\times$ model\_family interaction was not statistically significant
($F < 2.1$, $p > .05$ for all primary dimensions), confirming that condition differences
are protocol-level, not model-specific.
M4 (Llama-3.1-70B) showed a slightly higher exclusion rate on C2 ($10.4\%$ vs $4.1\%$
for M1) due to greater misconception-loss failure; this is reported as a limitation.

\subsection{Cross-Dataset Replication: Bridge (H4)}

Replicating the main experiment on 80 Bridge scenarios, protocol effects were directionally
consistent in 83\% of primary comparisons (pre-registered threshold: $\geq 70\%$; H4 supported).
Effect sizes were attenuated on Bridge ($d_{\text{mean}} = 1.8$ vs $d_{\text{mean}} = 3.4$
on MathDial), consistent with Bridge's greater diversity of tutoring styles and error-type
distributions (reported in Discussion).

\subsection{Protocol-Element Ablations (H5)}

Removing each of the 8 structural elements produced a statistically significant reduction
($p < .05$, uncorrected) in its target trace metric relative to the full protocol baseline.
The largest drops were:
\begin{itemize}
  \item C1\_ablate\_transfer: near-transfer attempt rate $\Delta = -0.91$ ($p < .001$)
  \item C2\_ablate\_misconception: target-error preservation $\Delta = -0.67$ ($p < .001$)
  \item C2\_ablate\_gradual: correction-timing index $\Delta = -0.44$ ($p = .002$)
  \item C1\_ablate\_questions: question-asking rate $\Delta = -0.73$ ($p < .001$)
\end{itemize}
This confirms that structural requirements --- not surface prompting --- drive the observed
condition differences (H5 supported).

\subsection{Qualitative Failure Analysis}

Across all conditions, six failure categories were identified at varying rates (Table~\ref{tab:failures}).
C4 showed the highest premature-correctness rate ($M = 1.00$), confirming the competence

misconception-loss rates, while C1 showed the lowest premature-correctness ($M = 0.031$),
demonstrating that protocol constraints actively suppress competence leakage.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Protocol-Level Differences Matter for Educational Research}

Our results provide strong evidence that TA and SS protocols are not interchangeable
LLM prompts. Under matched public tutoring cases, they produce structurally different
simulated-learner evidence that is purpose-appropriately distinguishable by human coders
with high reliability, by automatic trace metrics across 4,800 dialogues, and across
multiple LLM families and datasets.

The core insight is that protocol failure modes are protocol-specific. C1 fails when the
learner becomes prematurely expert (suppressed but not eliminated by the student-stance
requirement); C2 fails when the target misconception is not preserved (mitigated but not
eliminated by explicit injection). These failures are not detectable by generic
``sounds like a student'' evaluation criteria. They require framework-aligned coding
against the purposes the protocols are designed to serve.

\subsection{LLM Validity Threats Are Protocol-Mediated}

The competence paradox \citep{yuan2026} manifests differentially across conditions.
C4 (no-role assistant) shows universal premature correctness, confirming that unstructured
LLMs default to expert behaviour. C1 and C2's structural constraints actively suppress
this: C1's ``do not solve the problem before the tutor has guided you'' and C2's explicit
misconception injection both function as epistemic fidelity constraints that are partially
effective across all 4 LLM families.

The finding that condition $\times$ model interactions are non-significant has important
practical implications: researchers can use TA or SS protocols with reasonable confidence
that their choice of LLM family will not systematically alter the form of evidence produced,
within the boundaries established by our model set.

\subsection{Cross-Dataset Attenuation: A Substantive Finding}

The attenuation of effect sizes on Bridge relative to MathDial (mean $d = 1.8$ vs $3.4$)
is an informative rather than disqualifying finding. Bridge dialogues represent more
diverse tutoring styles (expert vs. novice contrast built into the corpus structure),
and its error-type taxonomy does not map perfectly onto MathDial's (D8 mapping table).
This attenuation suggests that protocol effects are genuine but partially moderated by
the specificity and consistency of the underlying tutoring corpus --- a finding that
itself has practical implications for researchers selecting scenario seeds.

\section{Implications for Educational Technology Design}

Based on our quantitative findings and practitioner perspectives, we offer the following
evidence-grounded design recommendations for researchers using LLM-based simulated learners:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Match protocol to purpose.} Researchers designing learning-by-teaching
    environments should use C1-style (TA) protocols evaluated on TA1--TA4; researchers
    building diagnostic or feedback-policy testbeds should use C2-style (SS) protocols
    evaluated on SS1--SS5. Using the wrong protocol for the purpose introduces systematic
    evidence invalidity.

    \item \textbf{Never use generic student-role prompting for research.} ``Act as a math
    student'' prompting (C3) fails to achieve either learning-by-teaching or
    diagnostic-simulation purposes in 78\% of dialogues. It is not a safe default.

    \item \textbf{Inject misconceptions explicitly for SS protocols.} The single largest
    driver of SS2 (error-model alignment) is explicit misconception injection from metadata.
    Without it, misconception loss rate rises by 67 percentage points. When designing SS-style
    prompts, the specific target misconception must be part of the system prompt.

    \item \textbf{Include a transfer probe for TA protocols.} Near-transfer attempt rate
    drops from 1.00 to 0.09 when the transfer requirement is ablated. The transfer probe
    is the primary structural mechanism producing the TA protocol's key distinctive evidence.

    \item \textbf{Test with multiple LLM families before deployment.} Our cross-model
    robustness results show that protocol effects generalise, but M4 (Llama-3.1-70B)
    shows higher exclusion rates on C2. Open-source models should be validated on a pilot
    before full-scale educational deployment.

    \item \textbf{Report which protocol and evaluation framework you used.} Research
    papers using LLM simulated learners should name the protocol (TA-type or SS-type or
    neither), report the structural elements included, and evaluate against the
    purpose-appropriate framework dimensions. Generic ``student-like'' quality ratings are
    insufficient for research validity claims.

    \item \textbf{Use our use-case decision framework as a validation tool.} The use-case
    decision analysis (human coders classify dialogue by appropriate educational use without
    seeing condition labels) can serve as a protocol-independent validity check. A dialogue
    coded as ``reject'' by this criterion is invalid for any of the four educational uses
    regardless of its surface quality.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Ethical and Equity Considerations}

\subsection{Replication of Dataset Biases}

MathDial was collected primarily with US students; Bridge includes mixed international
tutor-student pairs; Eedi QATD-2k reflects UK school students (approximately age 12).
Our simulated learners inherit implicit biases embedded in these training corpora through
the LLMs that generated them. In particular, our ``7th-grade student'' representation
may reflect middle-class, English-speaking, Western educational norms and may not
accurately represent learners from other SES backgrounds, linguistic communities, or
educational contexts. Researchers applying these protocols in diverse or non-Western
contexts should conduct additional validation.

\subsection{RLHF Alignment Biases}

All four LLM families use reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), which has
been shown to reflect annotator demographic biases \citep[see, e.g., Gebru et al., 2021]{gebru2021}.
Our models may systematically underrepresent student reasoning styles that differ from
the RLHF annotator population. This is a limitation of all current LLM-based simulation
research and should be addressed as more diverse RLHF processes become available.

\subsection{Boundaries of LLM-Based Student Simulation}

LLM-based simulated learners cannot and should not replace real-student research for claims
about human learning outcomes. Our study evaluates what simulated learners can provide
as \emph{research instruments} (a controlled testbed for tutoring strategies, a validation
tool for learning-by-teaching environments) rather than as proxies for real learning.
The failure rates documented in our qualitative analysis --- particularly misconception loss
and premature correctness --- represent validity threats that any research using these
protocols must monitor and report.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Limitations}

This study has several important limitations. \textbf{Domain scope}: All scenarios are drawn
from K-7 math word problems; protocol differences may not generalise to other domains
(science, history, writing), other age groups, or non-English-language tutoring.
\textbf{Model coverage}: Despite testing four LLM families, our model set is limited to
English-language frontier models with RLHF alignment. Smaller models, non-English models,
or models without instruction tuning may show different patterns.
\textbf{Operational framework definitions}: Our TA1--TA4 and SS1--SS5 operationalisations
are derived from published frameworks but have not been through full psychometric scale
validation. The Codebook v1.1 dimensions should be treated as research instruments
appropriate for this study's purposes, not validated psychometric scales.
\textbf{Ecological validity}: We do not study whether our protocols produce dialogues that
improve actual student learning outcomes in LBT or ITS contexts. This boundary is explicitly
stated in our claim boundaries.

\section{Conclusion}

This study provides a methodological and evidential foundation for purpose-specific
evaluation of LLM simulated learners. We demonstrate that Teachable Agent and Student
Simulation protocols produce structurally different forms of evidence for different
educational purposes, that these differences are stable across four LLM families and two
tutoring datasets, and that unstructured student-role prompting fails to reliably achieve
either purpose.

For practitioners: match your protocol to your purpose, inject misconceptions explicitly
for SS protocols, include transfer probes for TA protocols, and never use generic
student-role prompting for research.

For the field: protocol-level evaluation using purpose-aligned frameworks is a necessary
complement to surface-level authenticity judgements for LLM student simulation research.
As LLM-based simulated learners become more prevalent in educational technology development,
the framework we provide offers a principled basis for distinguishing valid from invalid uses.
