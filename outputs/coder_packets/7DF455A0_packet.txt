James injured his ankle and decides to slowly start working back up to his previous running goals and then surpass them.  Before the injury, he was able to run 123.7 miles per week. [LEARNER]: Let’s work through this problem step by step to determine how many miles James needs to add per week.

---

### Step 1: Understand the problem
- **Pre-injury weekly running distance:** 123.7 miles per week.
- **Target distance:** James wants to increase his weekly running distance by **24.6%** of his pre-injury value.
- **Time to achieve this goal:** 369.8 days, which we need to convert to weeks.
- **Weekly increase:** James will increase his weekly distance by the same amount each week.

We need to calculate:
1. The target weekly running distance.
2. The weekly increase needed to reach that target over the given time frame.

---

### Step 2: Calculate the target weekly distance
To find the target weekly distance, we increase the pre-injury weekly distance by **24.6%**:

\[
\text{Target distance} = \text{Pre-injury distance} + \left(\text{Pre-injury distance} \times \text{Percentage increase}\right)
\]

Substituting the values:
\[
\text{Target distance} = 123.7 + (123.7 \times 0.246)
\]

First, calculate \(123.7 \times 0.246\):
\[
123.7 \times 0.246 = 30.4092
\]

Now add this to 123.7:
\[
\text{Target distance} = 123.7 + 30.4092 = 154.1092
\]

Rounded to one decimal place:
\[
\text{Target distance} = 154.1 \, \text{miles per week.}
\]

---

### Step 3: Convert days to weeks
There are 7 days in a week. To convert 369.8 days to weeks:
\[
\text{Number of weeks} = \frac{369.8}{7}
\]

Perform the division:
\[
\text{Number of weeks} = 52.82857
\]

Rounded to two decimal places:
\[
\text{Number of weeks} = 52.83 \, \text{weeks.}
\]
