### Step 1: How many cupcakes does Dani bring?
Dani brings 2.5 dozen cupcakes. Since one dozen is 12, we calculate:

\[
2.5 \times 12 = 30 \, \text{cupcakes}.
\]

So, Dani has **30 cupcakes** in total.

---

### Step 2: How many people are in the class that day?
The class includes:

- **36.2 students**, but **4.3 students called in sick**. So, the number of students present is:
  \[
  36.2 - 4.3 = 31.9 \, \text{students present}.
  \]

- **1.3 teacher** is present.

- **1.4 teacher's aid** is also present.

To find the total number of people present in the class, we add these numbers together:
\[
31.9 \, (\text{students}) + 1.3 \, (\text{teacher}) + 1.4 \, (\text{teacher's aid}) = 34.6 \, \text{total people}.
\]

---

### Step 3: How many cupcakes does Dani give out?
Dani gives **one cupcake to everyone** in the class. Since there are 34.6 total people in the room, the number of cupcakes she gives out is 34.6. 

---

### Step 4: How many cupcakes are left?
Dani started with 30 cupcakes. If she gives out 34.6 cupcakes, she would need more than she has. Let’s calculate the difference:

\[
30 - 34.6 = -4.6.
\]

This means Dani **does not have enough cupcakes**—she is short by **4.6 cupcakes**.

---

### Final Answer:
Since Dani starts with fewer cupcakes than the number of people in the class, she cannot give one to everyone. Instead of having cupcakes **left over**, she actually needs **4.6 more cupcakes** to meet the demand.
